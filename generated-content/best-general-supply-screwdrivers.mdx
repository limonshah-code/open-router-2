## What to Look for Before Buying the Best General Supply Screwdrivers

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
The first step is honestly assessing your primary tasks. Are you working on delicate electronics with tiny Torx screws? Then a precision set with micro bits is essential. For household furniture and appliances, standard Phillips and slotted sizes in medium lengths will suffice. Mechanics need ratcheting and offset drivers for confined spaces. Consider the screw types you encounter most often—Phillips, slotted, Torx, hex—and ensure the set covers those. Also think about access: deep recessed screws require extended shafts, while cramped areas may demand swivel heads or offset ratchets. A mismatch here leads to stripped screws and frustration.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations
General supply screwdrivers range from $10 for basic 5-piece sets to $40+ for comprehensive 18-piece collections with cordless options. For occasional home use, $15-$25 gets a solid set covering most needs. Professionals or frequent hobbyists should budget $25-$40 for premium materials, ratcheting mechanisms, and extensive bit variety. Cordless models add $5-$10. Avoid the cheapest no-name sets under $10—they often use poor steel that strips easily and breaks. Investing $20-$30 in a reputable brand like General Tools ensures tools that last years rather than months.

### Key Features - Most important technical specifications
Prioritize these features based on your needs: bit material (chrome-vanadium or 8650 alloy steel for durability), bit finish (black oxide or chrome plating to resist corrosion), handle design (cushioned non-slip grips for comfort and control), and retention mechanism (tight locking chucks prevent bit wobble). For cordless models, check RPM (100+ is good) and battery type (standard AAAs are convenient). Bit variety matters—look for sets that include Torx and hex if you work on modern electronics. Swivel heads and ratcheting mechanisms are valuable additions for tight spaces.

### Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction
Examine the steel: heat-treated alloy steels like 8650 are much tougher than cheap carbon steel. Blades should be one-piece forged, not welded, to prevent breakage at the joint. Chrome plating adds corrosion resistance. Handles should be solidly attached to the blade with no wobble. For sets with interchangeable bits, the chuck must grip bits firmly—test by shaking; bits should not rattle. General Tools uses reliable materials, but always check product images for solid construction. Durability often correlates with weight; ultra-light tools may sacrifice strength.

### Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers
Sticking with established brands like General Tools, Craftsman, or Klein Tools is wise. These companies have decades of experience, consistent quality control, and often offer warranties. They invest in proper metallurgy and ergonomics. Unknown brands may copy designs but use inferior steel and heat treatment, leading to early failure. Established brands also have better customer service and replacement part availability. For a tool you'll use regularly, the small premium for a reputable name pays off in longevity and performance.

### Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage
If you work on-site or travel light, consider size and storage. Single-tip drivers are most portable but least versatile. Compact sets with zippered cases (like the 18-piece) fit in a backpack. Cordless models add bulk but eliminate cord hassle. Ratchet handles are typically longer. Evaluate your workspace: a bulky set may be fine for a permanent bench, but a field technician needs slim profiles. Also consider bit storage—cases with individual slots prevent loss better than bulk compartments. Weight matters if you'll carry tools all day.

For interchangeable bits, ensure the chuck is clean so bits seat properly. Avoid using screwdrivers as pry bars, which can bend blades. Periodically check handle tightness if they're assembled. With proper care, quality screwdrivers can last decades.
