## What to Look for Before Buying the Best Inverter Generator

### Find Your Need - Compatibility and use case considerations
Start by calculating your actual wattage needs. List every device you plan to run simultaneously and note both its running watts and higher starting (surge) watts. A refrigerator might use 600W running but needs 1200W to start. For RVs, consider your air conditioner's startup surge. For home backup, prioritize essentials like fridge, freezer, sump pump, and lights. Underestimating leads to frustration, while overbuying wastes money and adds unnecessary weight and noise. A 2000-3000W unit often suffices for small appliances and electronics, while whole-home backup may require 5000W+.

### Budget - Setting realistic price expectations
Inverter generator prices scale dramatically with power, features, and brand. You can find capable 2000-3000W models between $400-$700. Mid-range dual-fuel units (3000-5000W) typically run $600-$1200. Large capacity or tri-fuel models with home backup features start around $1500 and can exceed $3000. Set a budget that reflects your required wattage and need for features like remote start, 240V outlets, or multi-fuel capability. Remember, a higher upfront cost often buys better reliability, quieter operation, and longer lifespan.

### Key Features - Most important technical specifications
* **Wattage (Running & Peak/Surge):** The most critical spec. Ensure running watts cover your continuous needs and peak watts cover the highest startup demand.
* **Total Harmonic Distortion (THD):** Should be under 3% for safe use with sensitive electronics like computers and medical devices.
* **Noise Level (dBA):** Measured at a standard load. Below 60 dBA is considered very quiet, 60-70 dBA is moderate. Open-frame models are louder.
* **Fuel Type & Flexibility:** Gas is common; propane burns cleaner and stores longer; some offer tri-fuel (gas/propane/natural gas) for ultimate flexibility.
* **Outlets:** Ensure you have the types and quantity you need (120V 5-20R, 240V L14-30R, TT-30R RV, USB).
* **Runtime & Fuel Tank:** Check runtime at 50% load. Larger tanks mean less frequent refueling but add weight.

### Quality & Durability - What to look for in construction
Look for signs of robust engineering: a solid, rigid frame; quality wheels and a sturdy handle for portability; and a well-sealed control panel. The engine is heart—brands like Honda, Westinghouse, and Briggs & Stratton have proven durability. Check the warranty; a 3-year limited warranty is standard for good brands and indicates confidence. Metal construction over plastic in critical areas, cast iron cylinder sleeves, and automatic low oil shutdown are durability and safety hallmarks.

### Brand Reputation - Importance of established manufacturers
Sticking with established brands like Honda, Westinghouse, WEN, Yamaha, and Champion pays off. These companies have mature engineering, reliable supply chains for parts, and extensive dealer/service networks. Their products undergo rigorous testing, adhere to strict emissions (EPA) and safety (CARB, UL) standards, and hold better resale value. A no-name brand might save $100 initially but risks poor support, scarce parts, and shorter lifespan, costing more long-term.

### Portability/Size - Considerations for space and storage
Consider where you'll store and use the generator. Weight and dimensions matter. Units under 100 lbs with wheels and a telescoping handle are manageable for one person. Larger 5000W+ models often weigh 200-300+ lbs and are best for fixed installations or require two people to move. For RV or car camping, size and weight are paramount. For a permanent home backup shed, size is less critical than power and features.
